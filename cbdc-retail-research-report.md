# Comprehensive CBDC Retail Adoption Report
## Global Analysis of Central Bank Digital Currency Implementation

**Report Date:** March 2026  
**Research Focus:** Retail CBDC Adoption Worldwide

---

## Executive Summary

Central Bank Digital Currencies (CBDCs) have emerged as a significant innovation in the global financial landscape, with 134 countries (representing 98% of global GDP) now exploring some form of CBDC implementation. However, retail adoption has been markedly uneven across jurisdictions. While China's e-CNY has achieved substantial scale with over $2 trillion in transaction volume, most other retail CBDCs have struggled to gain meaningful traction, with circulation typically representing less than 0.5% of total currency in circulation.

This report analyzes eight major retail CBDC initiatives, examining adoption metrics, success factors, and lessons learned from both high-performing and struggling implementations.

## 2. Detailed Country Analysis

### 2.1 China - e-CNY (Digital Yuan)

**Launch:** Pilot began in 2020, now covering 26 localities across 17 provinces

**Key Statistics (as of September 2025):**
- **Wallets:** 225 million personal wallets opened on the official app (2.25 billion total wallets including multiple per user)
- **Transaction Volume:** ¥14.2 trillion (~$2 trillion USD) cumulative
- **Transactions:** 3.32 billion transactions processed
- **Average Transaction Value:** ¥428 (~$60) - indicating small retail usage
- **Coverage:** 29 cities for everyday payments

**Key Features:**
- Offline payment capability (crucial for remote areas)
- Integration with popular apps (WeChat Pay, Alipay)
- "Red packets" (digital envelopes) for stimulus distribution with smart contracts
- Dual-center architecture: International operations center in Shanghai, management center in Beijing

**Adoption Drivers:**
- Government promotion and stimulus campaigns
- Deep integration with existing digital payment infrastructure
- Strong merchant acceptance through existing QR code systems
- Cross-border testing via mBridge project with Thailand, UAE, and Hong Kong

**Challenges:**
- Still only represents ~0.16% of China's M0 money supply
- Competition from established Alipay and WeChat Pay ecosystems
- Privacy concerns among citizens

---

### 2.2 Nigeria - eNaira

**Launch:** October 2021 (Africa's first retail CBDC)

**Key Statistics:**
- **Wallets:** 13 million+ created, but 98.5% inactive (IMF data)
- **Circulation:** 12.53 billion naira (~$8 million) by early 2024
- **Total Transaction Value:** ₦29.3 billion (~$66 million) since inception
- **Unbanked Users:** 33% of eNaira users previously lacked bank access

**Barriers to Adoption:**
- Weak digital infrastructure
- Limited public trust in government-backed digital systems
- Strong competition from cash and mobile money
- Cash withdrawal restrictions created backlash
- Limited merchant network

**Recent Developments:**
- Partnership with tech firm Gluwa to improve infrastructure
- Central Bank reassessing rollout strategy for 2025
- Plans to boost practical usage through revised incentives

---

### 2.3 Bahamas - Sand Dollar

**Launch:** October 2020 (World's first retail CBDC)

**Key Statistics (as of December 2024):**
- **Wallets:** 135,000+ registered (approximately 30% of population)
- **Merchants:** 2,034 registered merchant wallets
- **Circulation:** ~$1.1-1.4 million
- **Currency in Circulation:** <0.5% of total Bahamian currency
- **Financial Institutions:** 7 active participating AFIs

**Key Features:**
- Three-tier wallet system (Basic: $500 limit, Premium: $8,000 limit, Business: custom limits)
- No transaction fees for consumers
- Real-time settlement
- Designed for financial inclusion across islands

**Adoption Challenges:**
- Low merchant participation
- Limited integration with traditional banking system
- Commercial banks slow to adopt
- Customer education gaps
- Cash remains preferred for informal economy

**Strategic Response:**
- Central Bank preparing regulations to mandate commercial bank participation
- Wallet 2.0 upgrade planned with improved features
- Focus on government payment integration

### 2.4 Jamaica - JAM-DEX

**Launch:** July 2022

**Key Statistics (End of 2024):**
- **Wallets:** 282,274 registered users (~10% of population)
- **Growth:** 7% increase from 2023
- **Circulation:** J$258.5 million (~$1.7 million USD)
  - Public holdings: J$144.8 million (56%)
  - Wallet Providers: J$113.7 million (44%)
- **Merchants:** ~4,700 (90 small merchants, 4,500+ micro merchants)
- **Currency Share:** ~0.11% of total currency in circulation

**Incentive Programs:**
- Initial: J$2,500 (~$16 USD) to first 100,000 registrants
- 2023: Loyalty program with 2% cashback for customers
- Merchant incentive: J$25,000 to first 10,000 merchants

**Key Challenges:**
- Cash dominance (80% of transactions still cash-based)
- Limited bank participation (only National Commercial Bank fully functional)
- POS infrastructure requiring upgrades
- Limited customer interest beyond initial incentives

**Lessons:**
- Initial financial incentives alone don't guarantee sustainable adoption
- Merchant onboarding is critical bottleneck
- Need for integrated POS solutions rather than separate devices

### 2.5 India - Digital Rupee (e₹-R)

**Launch:** Pilot began December 1, 2022

**Key Statistics (March 2025):**
- **Users:** 6-7 million retail users
- **Merchants:** 420,000
- **Circulation:** ₹1,016.5 crore (~$122 million USD)
- **Growth:** 334% increase year-over-year (from ₹234 crore in 2024)
- **Daily Transactions:** ~100,000 (peaked at 1 million/day in Dec 2023)
- **Participating Banks:** 17 banks in retail pilot

**Key Features:**
- Offline payment capability (launched 2025)
- Programmability for government benefit transfers
- UPI QR code interoperability
- Integration with non-bank payment apps (Google Pay, PhonePe, CRED)

**Use Cases:**
- Odisha Subhadra Yojana: 88,000 women received programmable CBDC benefits
- Carbon credit transfers to farmers
- Kisan Credit Card loans
- Employee allowances (meal/fuel)

**Challenges:**
- Competition from highly successful UPI system (20+ billion monthly transactions)
- Still represents <0.003% of total currency in circulation
- Users prefer UPI for everyday transactions
- No interest accrual on CBDC holdings

**Strategic Advantages:**
- Built on existing robust digital payment infrastructure
- Focus on use cases where CBDC offers unique value (programmability, offline)
- Gradual expansion approach

### 2.6 Brazil - Drex

**Status:** Advanced pilot, targeting early 2026 launch

**Key Characteristics:**
- Not a classic CBDC but tokenized deposit infrastructure
- Built on distributed ledger technology
- Enables smart contracts and programmable finance
- Focus on financial inclusion through tokenized credit

**Participants:**
- Visa, Mastercard, Santander, Microsoft
- Multiple banks and fintechs

**Strategic Context:**
- Builds on Pix success (63 billion transactions in 2024, 155+ million users)
- Addresses $55 billion stablecoin market in Brazil
- Tokenized asset pilots generated $387.1 million in 2024

**Key Innovation:**
- Tokenized lending for SMEs and individuals
- Fractional asset ownership
- Integration with Brazil's Open Finance ecosystem

---

### 2.7 Eastern Caribbean - DCash

**Launch:** Pilot March 2021 (first CBDC for a currency union)

**Key Statistics:**
- **Participating Institutions:** 21 financial institutions (10 credit unions, 11 commercial banks)
- **Merchants:** ~400 by March 2023
- **Circulation:** EC$2.45 million (~$0.9 million USD) by March 2023
- **Currency Share:** 0.16% of total currency in circulation
- **Coverage:** 8 ECCU countries

**Major Setback:**
- 10-week platform outage in early 2022 significantly damaged trust

**Lessons Learned:**
- Importance of user education
- Need for merchant network development
- Integration with existing POS systems critical
- Technical resilience paramount

**Next Steps:**
- DCash 2.0 development underway (18-24 month timeline)
- Request for Vendor Information issued December 2023
- Focus on user-centric design

## 3. Key Insights on Adoption Patterns

### 3.1 Scale Disparities

The data reveals dramatic disparities in CBDC adoption:

| Tier | Countries | Characteristics |
|------|-----------|-----------------|
| **High Scale** | China | 100M+ users, trillions in volume, integrated with major payment systems |
| **Moderate Scale** | India, Nigeria | Millions of users but low activity rates, early pilot stages |
| **Low Scale** | Bahamas, Jamaica, ECCU | <1% of currency in circulation, limited merchant adoption |
| **Pre-Launch** | Brazil, EU | Advanced pilots, learning from others' experiences |

### 3.2 Common Adoption Patterns

**Early Surge, Then Plateau:**
Most CBDCs experience an initial surge in wallet creation driven by incentives (Jamaica's 100,000 registrations in weeks, Nigeria's 13 million wallets), followed by significant drop-offs in active usage.

**Low Circulation Ratios:**
Even the most successful retail CBDCs represent less than 0.5% of total currency in circulation, indicating that cash and traditional digital payments remain dominant.

**Wallet Inactivity:**
Nigeria's 98.5% wallet inactivity rate highlights a critical challenge: creating wallets is easier than generating sustained usage.

### 3.3 Regional Differences

**Asia-Pacific:**
- Focus on large-scale pilots with government backing
- Integration with existing super-app ecosystems
- China and India leading in absolute numbers

**Caribbean:**
- First-mover advantage but struggled with adoption
- Smaller populations allow for quicker saturation
- Financial inclusion primary driver

**Africa:**
- Nigeria's challenges demonstrate infrastructure barriers
- Competition from mobile money (M-Pesa model)
- Trust deficits with government digital systems

**Latin America:**
- Brazil learning from Pix success
- Focus on tokenization and financial innovation rather than just payments

## 4. Success Factors vs. Failure Factors

### 4.1 Critical Success Factors

Based on comparative analysis, the following factors correlate with higher adoption:

| Factor | High Performers | Low Performers |
|--------|----------------|----------------|
| **Existing Digital Infrastructure** | China's mature digital payments | Nigeria's limited infrastructure |
| **Integration with Popular Apps** | e-CNY + WeChat/Alipay | DCash requiring separate app |
| **Merchant Network** | China's ubiquitous acceptance | Jamaica's limited merchant onboarding |
| **Clear Value Proposition** | Brazil's tokenized credit focus | Bahamas' "digital cash" only |
| **Government Use Cases** | India's programmable benefits | Limited government adoption |
| **Private Sector Partnership** | Drex with major banks/fintechs | JAM-DEX with single bank |
| **Gradual Rollout** | India's phased expansion | Nigeria's rapid full launch |

### 4.2 Key Failure Factors

**1. Technology-First Approach**
Caribbean CBDCs demonstrate that building the technology without ecosystem development leads to low adoption. The ECCB admitted focusing on DCash development rather than implementation and use.

**2. Inadequate Merchant Onboarding**
All struggling CBDCs cite limited merchant participation as a primary barrier. Users need places to spend CBDC.

**3. Competition with Existing Systems**
- India: CBDC struggles against UPI's zero-cost, instant payments
- Caribbean: Cash remains king in informal economies
- China: e-CNY competes with deeply embedded Alipay/WeChat Pay

**4. Lack of Clear Differentiation**
When CBDC offers only what existing payment methods already provide (speed, low cost), users see no reason to switch.

**5. Trust Deficits**
- Privacy concerns (China)
- Government surveillance fears (global)
- Technical failures damaging confidence (DCash outage)

**6. Banking Sector Resistance**
Commercial banks often view CBDCs as competitive threats rather than opportunities, leading to limited distribution support.

## 5. Data-Driven Conclusions

### 5.1 The Adoption Paradox

CBDCs face an adoption paradox: **the countries with the strongest need for financial inclusion often lack the infrastructure to support CBDC adoption, while countries with robust infrastructure already have efficient payment alternatives.**

- Nigeria needs CBDC for inclusion but lacks infrastructure
- India has infrastructure but UPI already serves most needs
- Caribbean islands needed digital solutions but lacked scale

### 5.2 Critical Mass Requirements

Analysis suggests CBDCs require:

1. **Minimum 10% active user penetration** for network effects to begin
2. **1,000+ merchants per million population** for usability
3. **Government payment integration** to drive consistent volume
4. **Private sector distribution partnerships** for sustainable growth

Only China's e-CNY has achieved all four thresholds.

### 5.3 The Programmability Advantage

India's experience with programmable CBDC for government benefits (Odisha scheme) suggests **programmability may be CBDC's killer feature**. Use cases where CBDC can do something existing payment methods cannot:
- Targeted stimulus with spending restrictions
- Automated benefit distribution
- Conditional cash transfers
- Carbon credit payments

### 5.4 Infrastructure Before Currency

Brazil's Drex approach—building on Pix's success—suggests that **payment infrastructure development should precede CBDC launch**. Countries that first establish robust real-time payment systems (Pix, UPI) create a foundation for eventual CBDC integration.

### 5.5 The 0.5% Ceiling

No retail CBDC has exceeded 0.5% of total currency in circulation. This suggests either:
- Current CBDC designs don't meet user needs effectively
- The transition from cash/digital to CBDC requires generational change
- CBDCs may find their niche in specific use cases rather than general payments

### 5.6 Predictions for 2026-2030

Based on current trajectories:

| Country | Likely Outcome |
|---------|---------------|
| China | Continued scale growth, possible nationwide rollout |
| India | Moderate growth, focus on government/programmable use cases |
| Brazil | Successful Drex launch built on tokenization innovation |
| EU | Digital Euro decision by 2026, potential launch by 2028 |
| Nigeria | Continued struggle without infrastructure investment |
| Caribbean | DCash 2.0 and Sand Dollar 2.0 may see modest improvement |

## 6. Recommendations for Policymakers

### For Countries Considering CBDC Launch:

1. **Assess existing payment infrastructure**—CBDC may not be needed where efficient systems exist
2. **Identify unique use cases** where CBDC offers capabilities existing methods cannot
3. **Establish private sector partnerships** before launch, not after
4. **Plan for merchant onboarding** as a core project component
5. **Consider phased rollout** rather than immediate nationwide launch

### For Countries with Struggling CBDCs:

1. **Focus on interoperability** with existing payment systems
2. **Develop government payment use cases** to drive consistent volume
3. **Incentivize commercial bank participation** through regulatory requirements or benefits
4. **Invest in merchant infrastructure** (POS upgrades, training)
5. **Consider rebooting** with lessons learned (DCash 2.0 approach)
